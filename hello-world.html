Consul Hyrax
The Rock Ambassador • Africa

Small but fearless, the rock hyrax is the closest living relative to the elephant. They live in tight-knit colonies on rocky cliffs and have an astonishing vocabulary of whistles and alarm calls.

Fun fact: Despite their size, hyraxes have tiny tusks and sweat through their feet to stay cool on granite.

Our Ambassadors

About Hyrax & the Red-Footed Tortoise

Two very different creatures. One big shared mission: reminding the world to say hello.

🪨

Rock Hyrax

Procavia capensis • The “Rock Badger”

At first glance a chunky rabbit-sized mammal that suns itself on boulders. In reality, the rock hyrax is one of evolution’s most surprising plot twists.

Closest living relative of the elephant

Genetic studies show hyraxes share a common ancestor with elephants and manatees. They even have tiny tusks and a multi-chambered stomach like their giant cousins.

Life on the rocks

They live in colonies of 10–60 on rocky outcrops across Africa and the Middle East. Their feet have special sweat glands that keep them from slipping on smooth granite.

The language of the cliffs

Hyraxes are incredibly vocal. Different calls warn of eagles, leopards, or snakes. One “song” can last several minutes and sounds more like a bird than a mammal.

Why it matters: Hyraxes are ecosystem engineers. Their latrines fertilize thin soil on rocks, helping rare plants survive.
🐢

Red-Footed Tortoise

Chelonoidis carbonarius • The Painted Walker

Bright legs the color of sunset, a curious face, and a shell that tells the story of decades of slow adventure. The red-footed tortoise is one of the most charismatic reptiles in the Americas.

Vivid and varied

Their legs, head, and tail are splashed with red, orange, and yellow. No two tortoises have exactly the same pattern — each one is a walking work of art.

Slow wisdom

They can live 40–60 years in the wild (even longer in human care). They are mostly herbivorous and love fallen fruit and flowers.

Gentle neighbors

In many communities they are welcomed as “the tortoise that never bites.” They have excellent memory for routes to fruiting trees.

Conservation note: Habitat loss and the pet trade have reduced populations. Captive-breeding programs are helping future generations meet them.
